# Lab ground rules

Pre-analytical confounders, diurnal variation, phase dependencies, recheck intervals, and tracker-vs-lab tension patterns. Consult before evaluating any lab panel — these factors change how results are interpreted.

---

## Fasting requirements

Results are unreliable or non-comparable if these markers are drawn non-fasting.

| Marker | Fast required | Minimum fast | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Glucose | **Yes** | 10–12 h (water only) | Non-fasting glucose diagnostically worthless — even a small meal spikes it. |
| Insulin | **Yes** | 10–12 h | Postprandial insulin lingers 4–6 h. Fasting insulin requires true fast. |
| Lipids (TC, LDL, HDL, TG) | **Yes** | 10–12 h | Triglycerides peak ~4 h post-meal; non-fasting TG >150 is noise. Recent non-fasting panels are acceptable for non-HDL-C / ApoB trend but not TG. |
| Iron panel (Fe, TIBC, ferritin, saturation) | **Yes** | 8–12 h | Iron is absorbed rapidly from a meal — a single iron-rich breakfast can saturate transferrin and falsely normalise a deficient state. |
| Homocysteine | **Preferred** | 8+ h | Methionine load from a protein-rich meal elevates homocysteine. Fasting preferred for actionable interpretation. |
| Vitamin D | **No** | — | D stable over hours; fasting irrelevant. Fat-soluble, no diurnal variation. |
| B12 / Folate | **Preferred** | 8+ h | Oral B12 supplements spike serum B12 for 8–12 h. Draw before AM dose. |
| hs-CRP | **No** | — | Acute-phase reactant; fasting irrelevant, but recent infection or injury invalidates. |
| HbA1c | **No** | — | 90-day rolling average. Reflex glucose on the same draw needs fasting however. |

**Always ask:** "What time did you last eat or drink anything other than water?" If <8 h, flag every fasting-required marker as potentially unreliable.

## Diurnal variation

Many hormones follow circadian rhythms. The wrong draw time produces a misleading result.

| Marker | Peak window | Acceptable draw time | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cortisol (serum) | 6–9 AM | Before 10 AM | Drops ~50% by noon. An AM draw >10 AM underdiagnoses high cortisol. For HPA assessment, use 4-point salivary (AM, noon, PM, bedtime). |
| Cortisol (salivary) | Awakening response | Collect at wake, 30 min, noon, 4 PM, bedtime | Single AM salivary cortisol is uninformative — need the curve. |
| Testosterone (total & free) | 7–10 AM | Before 11 AM | Men lose 25–30% from AM peak to afternoon. An afternoon draw can misclassify someone as low. Women have smaller but real diurnal variation. |
| TSH | 2–4 AM (peak) | Before 10 AM | TSH is ~30–50% lower in afternoon draws. An "elevated" afternoon TSH may be normal AM. |
| fT4, fT3 | Stable | Any time of day | Minimal diurnal variation, but T3 drops slightly in prolonged fasting. |
| DHEA-S | Stable AM | Any consistent time | Minimal diurnal variation, but reliable only if DHEA supplementation has been halted 48 h. |
| Aldosterone / Renin | Early AM | Before 10 AM | Significant positional and diurnal variation. Draw seated or supine. |
| Growth hormone | Sleep onset (stages 3–4) | Not useful as random draw | GH is pulsatile; random draws are noise. Use IGF-1 as proxy. |

**Best practice for hormone assessment:** Schedule draws before 10 AM, within 2 h of waking. For cortisol curve, use 4-point salivary.

## Menstrual cycle phase dependencies

For female-identifying users, reference ranges for reproductive hormones are phase-dependent. A result "in range" for follicular may be abnormal for luteal.

| Marker | Best draw phase | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Estradiol | Follicular (day 3–5) for baseline | Luteal E2 can be 5× higher. A "normal" follicular result is low if drawn in luteal. |
| Progesterone | Mid-luteal (day 19–22 of 28-day cycle) | Ovulation confirmation requires luteal phase draw. Follicular progesterone is always low — drawing then tells you nothing. |
| LH, FSH | Follicular (day 3–5) | Mid-cycle surge makes interpretation impossible outside follicular. |
| SHBG | No phase dependency | Stable across cycle. |
| Free T | Follicular preferred | SHBG varies with estrogen — affects free T calculation. Follicular more stable. |

**If cycle phase is unknown**, mark all sex-hormone results as phase-indeterminate and less actionable. Recommend repeat draw at correct phase.

**Post-menopausal / HRT:** Stable levels. No phase dependency — but timing relative to HRT dose matters (draw just before next dose for trough).

## Pre-analytical variables

Factors that change lab values before the tube reaches the centrifuge.

| Variable | Affected markers | Magnitude | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Prolonged tourniquet** (>1 min) | Iron, LDH, potassium, total protein, albumin | +5–15% (hemoconcentration) | Release tourniquet within 1 min. If >1 min, re-draw or note on lab req. |
| **Fist clenching** | Potassium | +1–2 mmol/L (dramatic) | Keep arm relaxed. Do not pump fist. |
| **Exercise before draw** | CK, creatinine, AST, ALT, cortisol, GH | 2–10× elevation | Rest 15–30 min before draw. No strenuous exercise within 24 h of CK. |
| **Recent meal (<4 h)** | Glucose, triglycerides, iron, homocysteine | Trigs +100–500%; glucose +20–40% | Document last meal time. Fast for metabolic panel. |
| **Sleep deprivation** (<6 h prior night) | Cortisol, glucose, CRP, TSH | +10–50% on cortisol & CRP; glucose +5–15% | Flag if user slept poorly. Cortisol unreliable. |
| **Alcohol previous night** | GGT, AST/ALT, TG, glucose, cortisol | GGT most specific; glucose may elevate | Note if >2 drinks. GGT may stay elevated 7–10 days in heavy use. |
| **Hydration** (dehydrated) | Sodium, potassium, creatinine, BUN, albumin (+5–15%) | Hemoconcentration | Encourage normal water intake before draw. |
| **Caffeine (<2 h before)** | Cortisol (+30–50%), glucose, catecholamines | Significant acute spike | No coffee 2 h before draw if cortisol is on the panel. |
| **Supplements drawn just after dose** | B12, vitamin D, iron (oral supplements spike serum levels) | B12 can 2–10× normal; D shifts modestly; iron spikes | Draw before morning supplement dose. Flag if user took supplements <6 h before. |

**When in doubt, flag the confounder.** A redrawn value at correct conditions is worth more than an action on a confounded result.

## Recheck intervals

How long to wait before re-testing after starting an intervention. Testing sooner wastes money and may misinterpret natural fluctuation as a trend.

| Marker | Minimum recheck window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **HbA1c** | **12 weeks (3 months)** | Reflects average glucose over 90 days. Earlier rechecks are useless. |
| **Fasting insulin** | 2–4 weeks | Can respond quickly to carb restriction, IF, or metformin. |
| **Glucose (fasting)** | 2–4 weeks | Daily glucose may fluctuate from stress/illness — trend over weeks, not days. |
| **Lipids (TC, LDL, HDL, TG)** | 6–8 weeks | Respond to diet/lifestyle changes within 4–6 weeks. Allow a full menstrual cycle for stable comparison. |
| **ApoB, Lp(a)** | 8–12 weeks | Slower to change; Lp(a) is largely genetically determined. |
| **TSH, fT4, fT3** | 6–8 weeks after dose change | Pituitary-thyroid axis takes 4–6 weeks to reach new steady state after levothyroxine dose change. |
| **Vitamin D (25-OH)** | **3 months after loading** | 25-OH D half-life is ~15–25 days. Steady state takes 3 months after loading dose change. |
| **Homocysteine** | 4–6 weeks after supplement change | Responds to B12, folate, TMG, creatine within 4 weeks. |
| **hs-CRP** | 4–6 weeks after anti-inflammatory intervention | Diet, exercise, supplements (curcumin, fish oil) show meaningful change in 4–6 weeks. |
| **Iron panel (Fe, ferritin, saturation)** | 4–8 weeks after oral iron | Ferritin responds slowly (weeks). Saturation and iron respond in days — but ferritin is the actionable marker for stores. |
| **Ferritin** (post-iron infusion) | **3 months** | After IV iron, ferritin may be artefactually elevated for weeks due to acute-phase response — not reflective of true stores. |
| **Testosterone (total & free)** | 4–6 weeks after intervention | Clomiphene, HCG, TRT all take 4–6 weeks for new steady state. |
| **Cortisol (salivary curve)** | 2–4 weeks after stress intervention | Responds to sleep improvement, stress reduction, adaptogens. |
| **SHBG** | 6–8 weeks | SHBG responds to thyroid status, insulin, estrogen, diet changes — slowly. |
| **DHEA-S** | 4–6 weeks after DHEA supplementation | Measure trough (just before next dose). |
| **Estradiol, Progesterone (HRT)** | 6–8 weeks after dose change on HRT | Steady state after transdermal/oral HRT change. |
| **CoQ10 (plasma)** | 8–12 weeks after starting supplement | Slow to accumulate. |
| **Liver enzymes (ALT, AST, GGT)** | 2–4 weeks after removing insult | Can normalise quickly after alcohol cessation or NAC supplementation. |
| **Creatinine, GFR** | 2–4 weeks | Stable if the insult (dehydration, nephrotoxin) is removed. A small change may be lab noise. |
| **BNP / NT-proBNP** | 2–4 weeks | Responds to efpeglenatide / tirzepatide / decongestion. |

## Tracker-vs-lab tension patterns

Lab results are a snapshot. Wearable data is a continuous movie. When they disagree, the shared signal is usually more informative than either in isolation.

| Tracker pattern | Conflicting lab | Interpretation | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Low HRV** (trending down, >3 weeks) | Normal hs-CRP, normal ESR | **Not inflammation.** Likely overtraining, poor sleep, chronic stress, or alcohol. | Prioritise recovery: sleep extension, stress reduction, lower training load. No anti-inflammatory needed. |
| **High RHR** (+5–10 bpm above baseline, >2 weeks) | Normal TSH, normal fT4 | **Not hyperthyroid.** Likely deconditioning, overtraining, stress, or volume depletion. | Check sleep, training load, hydration. Avoid unnecessary thyroid workup. |
| **Poor sleep** (Oura/Whoop: <75 sleep score, chronic) | Normal 8 AM cortisol | **Circadian misalignment, not HPA dysfunction.** The timing is off but the system can still produce cortisol. | Phase advance (light, meal timing, early bed). Don't treat the "normal" cortisol as evidence of no problem. |
| **High HRV** (trending up) | Elevated inflammatory markers (hs-CRP, IL-6, ESR) | **Good recovery despite ongoing inflammation.** Positive sign — body is compensating. | Treat the inflammation, but note the good HRV suggests the intervention is working. |
| **High RHR + Low HRV + Poor sleep** | Normal bloodwork (all panels in reference range) | **Lifestyle strain.** Alcohol, chronic stress, insufficient recovery, or overtraining. | Labs rule out pathology. Don't chase more tests. Intervention: sleep, stress, alcohol reduction. |
| **Waking RHR not dropping** (5–10 bpm below daily average) | Normal thyroid, normal iron | **Recovery deficit.** The autonomic nervous system is struggling. | Reduce training or stress load. Check alcohol, sleep debt, and mental load. |
| **Deep sleep <10% of night** (<45 min) | Normal inflammatory labs, normal hormones | **Not a lab problem.** Sleep architecture issue independent of pathology. | Sleep hygiene: consistent schedule, temperature, light, no alcohol. |
| **Large glucose spikes** on CGM (postprandial >40 mg/dL swing) | Normal HbA1c | **Early dysregulation detectable by CGM, invisible on HbA1c.** HbA1c normalises spikes into a 90-day average. | Address the spikes (glucose disposal, meal composition, order). Don't reassure based on HbA1c. |
| **Resting RHR not returned to baseline after illness** | Normal CRP, normal CBC | **Protracted recovery.** Autonomic recovery lags behind lab normalisation. | Scale return to training. Labs don't license full effort yet. |
| **Body temperature** chronically low (<36.5°C fasting AM) | Normal TSH, normal fT3 | **Suspicious for low T3 syndrome / euthyroid sick syndrome**, or metabolic adaptation in deficit. | Don't dismiss as "normal thyroid." Check reverse T3, consider if prolonged caloric restriction. |
| **Sleep blood oxygen (SpO₂)** drops >3% from baseline | Normal CBC (Hb, Hct, RBC) | **Sleep-disordered breathing** likely (UARS / mild apnea) despite no anemia. | In-lab sleep study (WatchPAT or PSG), not more bloodwork. |

**Rule of thumb:** When labs and trackers disagree, the tracker is seeing the *trend* while the lab is a *snapshot*. Both are true — the tension is the signal. Labs rule out pathology; trackers guide lifestyle intervention. Use the convergence to decide where to act, and the divergence to avoid testing fatigue (more labs when the tracker says it's lifestyle).
