# Cross-system connections

Step 2 of the skill groups signal cards by biological system. That grouping is necessary but not sufficient — gaps in one system are usually downstream of a root cause sitting in another. This reference defines the known inter-system axes so the skill can treat a shared root as a single higher-order gap instead of scoring five isolated markers.

When two or more markers from the same axis both show gaps, flag the **axis** as the gap and name the root. The intervention targets the root; the individual markers are confirmation readouts.

---

## Axis 1 — Gut–immune–thyroid

**Root candidates**: malabsorption, intestinal permeability, autoimmune thyroiditis, chronic gut inflammation.

| Marker cluster | What it suggests |
|---|---|
| Low ferritin + high WBC (or high NLR) + borderline-low TSH | Malabsorption starving iron AND disturbing thyroid — gut is the shared root. |
| Low ferritin + normal iron but low MCV | Functional iron deficiency from low copper (ferritin high-normal but MCV low → check ceruloplasmin, not just iron). |
| High TPO antibodies + rising TSH + low ferritin | Autoimmune thyroiditis with absorption impairment — treat gut and thyroid together. |

**Intervention root**: gut integrity (remove triggers, support mucosal barrier), then recheck iron and TSH together.

---

## Axis 2 — Metabolic–inflammatory

**Root candidates**: insulin resistance, visceral adiposity, excess PUFA/ethanol, sedentary status.

| Marker cluster | What it suggests |
|---|---|
| High TG + high hs-CRP + high fasting insulin + low HDL | Insulin resistance is the root; inflammation and dyslipidemia are downstream. Treat the axis, not the markers. |
| High uric acid + high TG + high BP | Metabolic syndrome cluster — urate is a proxy for fructose load and endothelial stress. |
| High GGT + high TG + high fasting glucose | Hepatic insulin resistance / fatty liver — GGT is the early liver signal. |

**Intervention root**: carbohydrate restriction, zone-2 training, visceral-fat reduction. Recheck fasting insulin first (responds in 2–4 weeks); TG and hs-CRP follow.

---

## Axis 3 — HPA–gonadal

**Root candidates**: chronic stress, sleep deficit, overtraining, caloric restriction.

| Marker cluster | What it suggests |
|---|---|
| High PM cortisol + low free T + low DHEA-S | Chronic stress suppressing the HPT axis — androgen production drops under cortisol load. |
| Low free T + high SHBG + normal TSH | Not primary hypogonadism — high SHBG is binding free T; root may be insulin resistance or thyroid. |
| Low free T + high RHR + low HRV (tracker) | Overtraining / under-recovery driving both — not a testosterone problem to treat pharmacologically. |

**Intervention root**: stress load reduction, sleep extension, training-periodization. Recheck free T after cortisol normalizes — don't add exogenous testosterone onto an unaddressed stress root.

---

## Axis 4 — Mitochondrial–nutritional

**Root candidates**: deficient endogenous antioxidant capacity, statin use, age-related CoQ10 decline.

| Marker cluster | What it suggests |
|---|---|
| Low CoQ10 + low Mg + low selenium | Endogenous antioxidant capacity is underbuilt — three cofactors of the same electron-transport defense. |
| Low CoQ10 + on statin + rising CK | Statin-associated CoQ10 depletion driving myopathy risk — CoQ10 repletion is the root fix. |
| High GGT + low selenium + low CoQ10 | Oxidative load exceeding antioxidant defense — treat the deficiency, not just the enzyme. |

**Intervention root**: CoQ10 (ubiquinol) + Mg + Se as a triad, plus addressing the oxidative source (alcohol, statin, PUFA).

---

## Axis 5 — Sleep–metabolic

**Root candidates**: circadian misalignment, glucose dysregulation, autonomic imbalance.

| Marker cluster | What it suggests |
|---|---|
| Poor deep sleep (<15%) + high glucose variability (CGM) + rising fasting insulin | Glymphatic clearance and glucose disposal share the night — bad sleep drives both. |
| Low HRV during sleep + high RHR during sleep + high fasting glucose | Autonomic recovery failing AND glucose not normalizing overnight — sleep architecture is the root. |
| Late-evening eating + poor sleep + high TG | Late meals disrupt both sleep and lipid clearance — one behavioral root. |

**Intervention root**: circadian anchoring (light, meal timing, early bed, stop eating 3 h pre-sleep). Recheck glucose variability and deep-sleep % together.

---

## Axis 6 — Nutritional–methylation

**Root candidates**: MTHFR / MTR / MTRR variants, B-vitamin deficiency, high homocysteine.

| Marker cluster | What it suggests |
|---|---|
| High homocysteine + low B12 + low RBC folate | Methylation pathway impaired — root is B12/folate status, often MTHFR-driven. |
| High homocysteine + MTHFR C677T + normal B12/folate | Genetic methylation inefficiency — methylfolate > folic acid; TMG as adjunct. |
| Low ferritin + high homocysteine + low B12 | Combined iron and methylation deficiency — common in plant-based diets; treat both. |

**Intervention root**: methylfolate + B12 (methylcobalamin) + TMG, dosed against homocysteine recheck at 4–6 weeks.

---

## Using axes in Step 2

After assigning each signal card to a system, scan the axes above. If ≥2 markers from one axis are both out of optimal range, write the axis as the gap:

`[axis name] = ROOT GAP | markers: [list] | root candidate: [most likely] | confidence: [high/medium/low]`

Then in Step 3, score the axis as one P0/P1/P2 gap. The individual markers become the re-check panel that confirms the root is closing.
