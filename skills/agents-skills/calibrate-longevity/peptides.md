## BPC-157 (Body Protective Compound)

| Property | Detail |
|---|---|
| Mechanism | Promotes angiogenesis, upregulates GH receptor expression, modulates TGF-β signalling. Accelerates healing of tendons, ligaments, GI mucosa. |
| Typical dose | 250–500 µg twice daily (AM/PM) |
| Cycle | 4–6 weeks on, 2–4 weeks off |
| Route | Subcutaneous (near injury site or abdomen) or oral (for GI) |
| Stack notes | Often paired with TB-500 for synergistic soft-tissue repair |
| Interaction risks | May potentiate anticoagulants; theoretical interaction with VEGF inhibitors |

## Harm reduction & sourcing appendix

All peptides listed above are T3 (experimental — limited human data). This appendix covers practical risk mitigation regardless of the specific compound.

### Sourcing quality indicators

| Indicator | What to check |
|---|---|
| **Certificate of Analysis (CoA)** | Must specify peptide, purity (≥98% for research-grade), date, and the analytical method (HPLC-MS preferred). Reject CoAs that list only the name without quantitative purity. |
| **Third-party testing** | CoA from an independent lab (not the manufacturer). Batch-specific, not generic. Mass-spec (MALDI-TOF / ESI-MS) confirms molecular weight — a single peak at the expected mass rules out common adulterants. |
| **Purity claim** | ≥99% for pharmaceutical reference standards; ≥98% acceptable for most peptides. HPLC trace should show a single dominant peak (>98% area). Multiple peaks = contamination or degradation. |
| **Endotoxin testing** | <5 EU/mg (or declared as endotoxin-free). Anything unlabelled is suspect for injection. |
| **Appearance** | Lyophilised powder should be a white to off-white fluffy cake or powder. Discolouration, oiliness, or caking at room temperature indicates degradation or substandard product. |

| **Red flags** | **Reject if present** |
|---|---|
| No CoA available | Impossible to verify identity or purity |
| CoA from the vendor's own "lab" | Self-certification is not independent testing |
| Peptide arrives in solution (pre-mixed) | Stability is unknown; lyophilised peptides degrade rapidly in solution without proper formulation |
| Price significantly below market | T3 peptides require proper synthesis — too cheap means skipped QC |
| Vendor requires no prescription | Legal flag, though common in this space |

### Reconstitution best practices

| Step | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Water type** | Use only bacteriostatic water (0.9% benzyl alcohol) for multi-dose vials. Sterile water (no preservative) for single-use only — discard after 24 h. Use sterile 0.9% saline for certain peptides (check solubility). |
| **Dilution calculation** | Add mL = (mg of peptide / desired mg per mL). E.g. 5 mg vial reconstituted with 2 mL → 2.5 mg/mL. Mark the vial with concentration and date immediately. |
| **Gentle swirling** | Do not shake vigorously — peptide bonds can shear. Roll or swirl gently until dissolved. If foaming occurs, wait for bubbles to settle before drawing. |
| **Dissolution time** | Most peptides dissolve within 1–2 min with gentle swirling. If insoluble after 5 min at room temperature, the product may be degraded or not the advertised peptide. |
| **Sterile technique** | Wipe vial tops with 70% isopropyl alcohol before each puncture. Use a fresh syringe/needle for each draw. Never re-use needles or syringes. |
| **Large particles** | If you see visible particulates that don't dissolve with gentle warming, do not use — filter through a 0.22 µm sterile filter or discard. |

### Storage

| Condition | Guideline |
|---|---|
| **Lyophilised powder (long-term)** | Store at -20°C (freezer) in a dark, desiccated container. Stable for 12–24 months. |
| **Lyophilised powder (in-use)** | Room temperature (15–25°C) for up to 1 month in cool, dark, dry place. Avoid temperature cycling. |
| **Reconstituted (refrigerated)** | 2–8°C (refrigerator). Most peptides stable 7–14 days. Discard after 14 days unless stability data supports longer (BDC-157 is more stable; some peptides degrade within 7 days). |
| **Reconstituted (frozen)** | NOT recommended — ice crystals can shear peptides. Use only if specifically formulated for freezing. |
| **Light sensitivity** | Protect from UV and direct sunlight. Amber vials or opaque containers preferred. |
| **Transport** | Use an insulated container with an ice pack. Avoid temperature extremes and prolonged transit. |

### Injection technique

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Needle gauge** | 29–31 gauge, 0.3–0.5 mL (insulin syringe) for subcutaneous. 27–29 gauge for intramuscular (if applicable). |
| **Needle length** | Subcutaneous: 4–8 mm (5/16" or 1/4"). Intramuscular (rare for peptides): 12–16 mm (1/2" to 5/8"). |
| **Rotation sites** | Rotate injection sites systematically: abdomen (quadrants), alternating thighs, upper arms, gluteal. Keep a log of which site was used each day. |
| **Abdominal injections** | 2 inches from the navel. Avoid scar tissue, stretch marks, and the belt line (compression line). |
| **Pinch technique** | Pinch a fold of skin gently, insert at 45–90° (depending on needle length), inject slowly (5–10 seconds for 0.3 mL). Release pinch after needle is fully inserted but before withdrawing. |
| **Post-injection care** | Apply light pressure with a dry cotton ball (no alcohol — stings and may close the puncture prematurely). Do not massage. |
| **IM contraindication** | Do not inject into areas with visible veins, nerves, tendons, or bone. |

### Signs of adverse reaction

| Reaction | Likely cause | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Injection site redness, swelling, itching** | Local histamine response or minor contamination | Apply cold compress. Usually resolves in 24 h. If spreading or worsening >48 h, discontinue and assess. |
| **Persistent burning during injection** | High BA content, low pH solution, or poor product | Dilute further or switch bacteriostatic water brand. If burning persists with fresh product, discontinue. |
| **Flu-like symptoms** (fever, chills, body aches) | Pyrogenic contamination (endotoxin) | **Stop immediately.** Notify source. Seek medical attention if persistent. |
| **Nausea, headache, dizziness** | Peptide-specific side effect or low blood pressure | Lower dose by 50% and re-evaluate. If severe or persistent, discontinue. |
| **Anaphylaxis / severe allergic reaction** | Rare. Peptide or excipient allergy | Emergency medical attention. EpiPen if available. |
| **Prolonged bleeding at injection site** | Nicked vessel or anticoagulant interaction | Apply firm pressure. If on anticoagulants, use smaller gauge needle and hold pressure longer. |

### Contamination risks by source type

| Source type | Common risks | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| **Research chemical supplier** | Purity variability, mislabelled peptides, endotoxin contamination, heavy metals | Require current third-party CoA. Test a sample via HPLC-MS before use if running a long cycle. |
| **Compounding pharmacy** | More consistent purity, but batch variability still exists | Verify licence and GMP certification. Check for FDA/EMA compounding registration. |
| **Foreign / grey market** | No regulatory oversight. Risk of: wrong peptide, improper storage, bacterial contamination, filler substitution | Maximum skepticism. Always request and verify CoA. Never use if CoA cannot be produced. |
| **Peptide "bargain" lots** | End-of-synthesis tailings with higher impurity load | Reject. The risk of a truncated or mis-acetylated sequence is elevated. |

### General safety principles for T3 peptides

1. **Start low, go slow.** Begin at 25–50% of the typical dose for the first 3–5 days. Titrate up only if no adverse effects appear.
2. **Single-source only.** Do not combine two T3 peptides until you have tolerated each individually for at least 2 weeks. This avoids ambiguous attribution of side effects.
3. **Document everything.** Log date, dose, batch/lot number, injection site, and any subjective effects (positive or negative) for every dose. If an issue arises, you can trace it to a batch.
4. **Have a washout plan.** Know what the clearance time is (~5 half-lives). If you need to stop for an upcoming medical procedure or travel, plan the washout.
5. **No T3 peptide is "safe" — only "less unsafe".** The absence of reported harm in a small cohort means absence of evidence, not evidence of safety. Each cycle is a calculated risk.
6. **Keep emergency contacts.** Have a physician aware that you are using T3 compounds. In an emergency, paramedics need to know what is in your system — carry a card listing compounds, doses, and schedule.
7. **Rotate vials.** If using a multi-month cycle, reconstitute a fresh vial every 7–14 days. Do not use a vial that has been reconstituted for more than 14 days unless stability data says otherwise.
8. **Never share vials or syringes.** Cross-contamination risks are real and include bloodborne pathogens even with single-use vials.
9. **If in doubt, throw it out.** A cloudy solution, a suspicious CoA, a product that doesn't dissolve as expected — discard. The cost of a vial is trivial compared to a hospital visit.
10. **Align with the bio-orchestrator's philosophy:** No intervention is free. Every T3 compound has a risk budget. The goal is to spend that budget where the expected ROI is highest — never on curiosity alone.
